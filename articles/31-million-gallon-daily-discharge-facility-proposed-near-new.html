3.1 Million Gallon Daily Discharge Facility Proposed Near New Braunfels

February 14, 2026 By Hill Country Sentinel Staff

A proposed wastewater treatment facility near New Braunfels, capable of discharging up to 3.1 million gallons per day, has sparked significant concern among Comal County residents. The facility, tied to the planned Broken Cedar Ranch development, recently shifted its focus to this area after facing rejection in Helotes. If approved, the project would represent a major addition to regional infrastructure, with potential implications for both the environment and the local economy.

The proposal, currently under review for necessary permits from the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ), involves discharging treated wastewater into a tributary that feeds the Guadalupe River system. This river is a cornerstone of the region's identity, supporting a vibrant recreation-based economy that includes tubing, fishing, and tourism. Local business owners and environmental advocates worry that any impact on water quality could jeopardize the area's appeal as a destination, which generates millions in annual revenue for New Braunfels and surrounding communities.

Community opposition is gaining momentum, particularly on social media platforms where residents have organized to voice their concerns. Many cite the scale of the proposed discharge—3.1 million gallons daily—as a potential threat to the delicate balance of the Guadalupe River ecosystem. Posts and online petitions highlight fears of pollution, habitat disruption, and long-term damage to aquatic life, with some residents calling for a full environmental impact study before any permits are granted.

"We're not against development, but this river is our lifeline," said Maria Gonzalez, a local business owner who relies on river tourism for her kayak rental company.

"If the water quality suffers, so does our entire community. We need assurances that this won't turn into a disaster for us."

Representatives for Broken Cedar Ranch have stated that the wastewater facility would adhere to strict TCEQ regulations and utilize modern treatment technologies to minimize environmental harm. They argue that the project is essential to support the growth of the development, which is expected to bring new housing and economic opportunities to the region. However, specifics about mitigation measures and long-term monitoring plans remain limited at this stage, fueling skepticism among critics.

The debate over the facility comes at a time when Comal County is grappling with rapid population growth and the accompanying strain on infrastructure. While some local leaders see projects like Broken Cedar Ranch as a necessary response to housing demands, others caution against prioritizing development over environmental preservation. The Guadalupe-Blanco River Authority has indicated it will closely monitor the permitting process to ensure compliance with water quality standards.

As the TCEQ review continues, public input will play a critical role in shaping the outcome. A public hearing is expected to be scheduled in the coming weeks, providing an opportunity for residents to formally express their concerns. For now, the future of the 3.1 million gallon-per-day discharge facility remains uncertain, with the potential to reshape the environmental and economic landscape of New Braunfels and beyond.
